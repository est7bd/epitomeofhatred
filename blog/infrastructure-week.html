First Term Record — 2017–2020 infrastructure broken promise fact-checked

"Infrastructure Week" Became a Running Joke. Trump Promised to Fix America's Roads and Bridges. He Never Did.

Trump campaigned on a $1 trillion infrastructure plan — roads, bridges, airports, broadband, water systems. It was one of his signature populist promises, one that Democrats were also open to. Between 2017 and 2020, the Trump White House declared "Infrastructure Week" approximately a dozen times. Each time, it was drowned out by a self-created crisis. No infrastructure bill passed his first term. Biden passed the $1.2 trillion bipartisan infrastructure bill in November 2021. Trump, who had proposed the same thing for four years and failed to pass it, publicly criticized it and told Republicans to vote against it.

June 2017 — Infrastructure Week #1

White House declares first Infrastructure Week. Overtaken by: Comey testimony before the Senate Intelligence Committee about Trump asking him to drop the Flynn investigation. Infrastructure news coverage: minimal.

August 2017 — Infrastructure Week #2

Administration pushes infrastructure messaging. Overtaken by: Charlottesville, Trump's "very fine people on both sides" press conference, widespread condemnation from both parties.

May 2019 — Nancy Pelosi meeting

Trump meets with Pelosi and Schumer to discuss a $2 trillion infrastructure framework — broad bipartisan agreement. Overtaken by: Trump walks out of the meeting 3 minutes in, saying he can't work with Democrats while investigations continue. Infrastructure talks collapse.

2020 — Final year

Infrastructure Week attempts continue. COVID-19 pandemic consumes the year. No bill.

November 2021 — Biden

Biden signs the $1.2T Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act with bipartisan support — 13 House Republicans voted for it. Trump publicly said Republicans who voted for it should be "ashamed." The American Society of Civil Engineers had been grading US infrastructure C- for years. Biden did what Trump promised and failed to deliver.

The American Society of Civil Engineers has graded US infrastructure in the C- to D range for decades. More than 42,000 bridges are structurally deficient. Broadband access remains unavailable in large swaths of rural America. Water systems in Flint, Newark, and dozens of other cities have had lead contamination crises. These are not abstract problems — they affect property values, economic development, public health, and the daily lives of tens of millions of Americans. The $1 trillion infrastructure promise was popular across party lines. The failure to pass it for four years, followed by criticism of the person who actually passed it, is the Infrastructure Week legacy.
