Charlottesville: A Woman Was Killed by a White Supremacist. Trump Said There Were "Very Fine People on Both Sides."

On August 11-12, 2017, hundreds of white nationalists and neo-Nazis marched through Charlottesville, Virginia, carrying torches and chanting "Jews will not replace us." They clashed with counter-protesters. A white supremacist drove his car into a crowd of people, killing 32-year-old Heather Heyer and injuring 19 others. Three days later, Trump said "you also had people that were very fine people, on both sides." He has never changed this position. In 2019 he said he answered "perfectly." This is documented from the transcript.

The Unite the Right rally was organized by white nationalist Jason Kessler and Richard Spencer, and attended by David Duke, former KKK leader. Participants carried Nazi flags and Confederate battle flags. They chanted "Jews will not replace us," "Blood and soil" — a Nazi slogan — and "White lives matter." On the evening of August 11, hundreds of torch-carrying marchers walked through the University of Virginia campus, surrounding a small group of counter-protesters. The images were unmistakable: this was an organized white supremacist march on American soil. On August 12, a self-identified white supremacist named James Alex Fields Jr. drove his car into a crowd of counter-protesters on a Charlottesville street, killing Heather Heyer and injuring 19 others. Fields was convicted of first-degree murder and sentenced to life in prison.

Trump's Response — in Sequence.

August 12, 2017 — Day of the attack: Trump posted on Twitter urging people to "be united" and condemning "hatred, bigotry and violence on many sides, on many sides." He did not name white supremacists, neo-Nazis, or the Ku Klux Klan. He drew an equivalence between the organizers of the rally and those protesting them.

August 14, 2017 — Two days later: Under bipartisan pressure, Trump issued a more explicit condemnation, saying "racism is evil" and naming "the KKK, neo-Nazis, white supremacists, and other hate groups." This was the statement his defenders later cited as his "real" response.

August 15, 2017 — The Trump Tower press conference: Trump then held a press conference and walked back his August 14 statement entirely, returning to his original "both sides" framing and adding the phrase that defined his response:

"You also had people that were very fine people, on both sides. You had people in that group — excuse me, excuse me — I saw the same pictures as you did. You had people in that group that were there to protest the taking down of, to them, a very, very important statue and the renaming of a park from Robert E. Lee to another name."

— Donald Trump, Trump Tower press conference, August 15, 2017

Trump also defended Robert E. Lee as "a great general" at this press conference, drew comparisons to George Washington and Thomas Jefferson as slaveholders to argue removing Confederate statues was equivalent to removing the Founders, called counter-protesters "the alt-left," and blamed "both sides" for the violence in which a woman had been murdered. When a reporter noted that neo-Nazis had started the confrontation, Trump replied: "You had a group on one side that was bad and you had a group on the other side that was also very violent."

Who the "Very Fine People" Actually Were.

Trump claimed the "very fine people" he was referring to were ordinary protesters who simply wanted to preserve the Robert E. Lee statue — that they were separate from the neo-Nazis. This claim does not survive scrutiny. The rally was organized explicitly by white nationalists as a white nationalist event. The chants — "Jews will not replace us," "Blood and soil," "White lives matter" — were not the chants of concerned history buffs. David Duke was there and said publicly that the rally represented "the fulfillment of the promises of Donald Trump." After Trump's August 15 press conference, white nationalist websites celebrated his remarks and called them a defense of their movement. The Washington Post reported that white supremacist message boards lit up with approval. The idea that there were "fine people" present who were separate from the Nazi contingent requires believing that people who chose to attend and march alongside people carrying swastikas and chanting Nazi slogans were uninvolved bystanders.

Six members of the panel resigned citing Trump's Charlottesville response as a deciding factor.

He Never Changed It.

In April 2019, Biden launched his presidential campaign by citing Charlottesville as the defining moment that convinced him to run. Trump responded by saying: "If you look at what I said, you will see that that question was answered perfectly." He called Robert E. Lee "a great general." He said "you had many people in that group other than neo-Nazis and white nationalists." He did not express regret. He did not modify his position. His position in 2019 was the same as his position in 2017: there were fine people on both sides of a confrontation in which white supremacists killed a woman. This is documented in the transcript, in his own words, on video.
