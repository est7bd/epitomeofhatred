The Supreme Court Looks Ready to Let Republicans Throw Out Mail Ballots That Arrive After Election Day. The 2026 Midterms Could Change Overnight.

A Republican challenge to Mississippi's ballot grace period could stop states from counting on-time mail ballots that arrive after Election Day. More than a dozen states could be affected before the 2026 midterms — including military and overseas voters who mailed their ballots on time.

← all posts

The Supreme Court does not have to say the words “voter suppression” for the effect to be obvious. This week, the justices heard a Republican challenge to Mississippi's rule that counts mail ballots postmarked by Election Day if they arrive within five business days. Based on the oral argument, the Court appears ready to say those ballots do not count at all. If that happens, the ruling will not stay in Mississippi. It could reach more than a dozen states before the 2026 midterms — and voters who did exactly what the rules told them to do could still be thrown out after the fact.

The Case Is Narrow on Paper and Massive in Practice.

The case, Watson v. Republican National Committee, targets a Mississippi law that has long allowed ballots mailed by Election Day to count if they arrive within five business days. Republican groups argue that federal law requires every ballot to be both cast and received by Election Day. During arguments, several conservative justices sounded sympathetic to that reading. The Washington Post and The Guardian both reported that the Court appears poised to limit or eliminate these grace periods.

That sounds technical until you remember who uses mail voting under these rules: elderly voters, disabled voters, rural voters, overseas civilians, and military service members whose ballots often cannot physically arrive on Election Day no matter how early they try to send them. The legal theory is tidy. The real-world effect is that voters who followed the law when they mailed their ballot could still lose their vote because the Court changed the rule after the envelope was already in the system.

If the Court says “Election Day” means “must be in the clerk's office by then,” the people punished first will be the voters with the least control over when the mail gets there.

This Is Not Just About Mississippi.

The reason this matters right now is scale. The Washington Post reported that Mississippi is one of 14 states with some version of a post-Election-Day receipt period for mailed ballots. The challenge is framed as a federal-law question, which means a ruling against Mississippi could instantly threaten those other state rules too. Republican litigators would not need to reinvent the argument state by state. They would already have the Supreme Court's blueprint.

And the timing is not subtle. This is landing in the run-up to the 2026 midterms, exactly when Republicans are already using court fights, voter-file demands, and redistricting to shape the field before November. A mail-ballot ruling that narrows who gets counted would slot neatly into that broader project.

The States-Rights Crowd Is Asking the Court to Take the States Out of It.

One of the ugliest parts of this case is the contradiction. Conservatives love to talk about federalism when Washington is doing something they hate. But here the Republican position asks the Court to override how states have chosen to administer mailed ballots in their own elections. Justice Ketanji Brown Jackson raised that point directly in argument, noting that the Constitution gives states broad authority to run elections unless Congress clearly says otherwise. Republican litigators are asking the Court to read that authority as narrowly as possible — because it helps them now.

That means the same movement that says Washington should not dictate policy to states is telling the Supreme Court to do exactly that when the subject is counting votes. It is not about principle. It is about which principle gets you closer to power.
