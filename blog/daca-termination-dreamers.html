First Term Record — September 5, 2017 DACA Dreamers immigration fact-checked

Trump Tried to End DACA. Courts Stopped Him. 700,000 Dreamers Who Grew Up in America Were Left in Limbo for Four Years.

Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals protected approximately 700,000 people who were brought to the United States as children — who went to American schools, learned English as their primary language, built their lives here — from deportation. On September 5, 2017, the Trump administration announced it was ending the program, giving Congress six months to pass legislation. Congress failed. Courts blocked the termination. The Supreme Court ruled 5-4 in 2020 that the process was "arbitrary and capricious." DACA recipients spent four years not knowing whether they would be deported to countries they barely remembered.

📁 First Term Record — documented history

DACA was established by the Obama administration in 2012 through executive action after Congress repeatedly failed to pass the DREAM Act, which would have provided a legal pathway for people who came to the US as children. DACA recipients — commonly called Dreamers — had to meet strict eligibility criteria: they had to have arrived before age 16, have no serious criminal record, be enrolled in school or have graduated high school, and pay a $495 fee. The program did not provide a path to citizenship. It provided renewable two-year deferrals from deportation and authorization to work legally.

Attorney General Jeff Sessions announced the termination on September 5, 2017, arguing that DACA was an unconstitutional overreach of executive power and that immigration enforcement was Congress's domain. The administration gave Congress six months to pass permanent legislation. Multiple legislative attempts failed — including a bipartisan deal that Trump rejected in January 2018 because it included protections for people from what he called "shithole countries." Courts blocked the termination. The administration pushed the termination to the Supreme Court. In June 2020, the Court ruled 5-4 in Department of Homeland Security v. Regents of the University of California that the administration's rescission of DACA was "arbitrary and capricious" under the Administrative Procedure Act — not because DACA itself was constitutionally required, but because the process by which it was ended was legally defective.

During the four years of legal limbo, approximately 700,000 people — many of whom had lived in the United States for a decade or more, some since infancy — did not know from month to month whether they would be allowed to remain. They could not plan for the future. Some did not want to travel outside the US for fear of being unable to return. Many were afraid to report crimes, engage with government services, or assert legal rights. The human cost of manufactured uncertainty is difficult to quantify but not difficult to imagine: these were people who grew up American, whose deportation would have sent them to countries where many did not speak the language.
