First Term Record — October 7, 2019 Kurds Syria ISIS fact-checked

Trump Abandoned the Kurdish Forces Who Had Fought ISIS Alongside America. Turkey Invaded Within Days.

The Syrian Democratic Forces — primarily composed of Kurdish fighters — were America's most effective partners in the campaign to defeat ISIS in Syria. They lost more than 11,000 fighters fighting alongside US troops. They held tens of thousands of ISIS prisoners and detainees. On October 6, 2019, Trump called Turkish President Erdogan. On October 7, the White House issued a statement saying US troops would withdraw from northeast Syria. Turkey invaded three days later. The Kurdish region was overrun. Hundreds of Kurds were killed. Thousands of ISIS-linked detainees escaped in the chaos. US military commanders — both active duty and retired — publicly stated the decision was a betrayal.

Turkey had long sought to attack the Kurdish YPG forces in northeast Syria, viewing them as connected to the PKK — a Kurdish militant group designated as a terrorist organization by the US and EU. The US had restrained Turkey throughout the anti-ISIS campaign because Kurdish fighters were indispensable to defeating ISIS. When Trump effectively removed that restraint with his withdrawal announcement, he gave Erdogan what he had wanted for years. The decision was made after a single phone call. It was not run through the National Security Council. Defense Secretary Mark Esper later said he had been "blindsided." Senior US officials in the region had made promises to Kurdish commanders — explicit assurances of US support — that Trump's announcement voided overnight.

The most concrete consequence beyond the immediate human cost was the ISIS prisoner problem. Kurdish forces had been holding approximately 70,000 ISIS-linked detainees — fighters, family members, and followers in camps and prisons. When Turkey invaded and the Kurds redirected their forces to defend their territory, security at these facilities degraded. Multiple mass escapes were reported. The exact number of ISIS-linked individuals who escaped is not precisely known, but the DHS and CIA assessed that the Kurdish detention of ISIS fighters had been a significant degradation of the organization's operational capacity — and that disrupting it had real consequences for global terrorism risk.

Trump responded to criticism by saying the Kurds "didn't help us in the Second World War, they didn't help us with Normandy." This was factually correct in a way that was also spectacularly irrelevant — the Kurds were not a state actor in 1944 and were not parties to World War II. He also said the Kurds "were paid massive amounts of money and equipment to do the fighting" — they were not; they were provided weapons and air support, not cash payments. He called the abandonment "strategically brilliant."
