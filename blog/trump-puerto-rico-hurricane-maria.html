Hurricane Maria Killed Nearly 3,000 Americans in Puerto Rico. Trump Threw Paper Towels at Them.

Hurricane Maria made landfall in Puerto Rico on September 20, 2017 as a Category 4 storm, devastating the island's infrastructure. The official death toll was initially 64. An independent study commissioned by the Puerto Rican government found approximately 2,975 people died — making it the deadliest natural disaster in the United States in a century. Trump visited two weeks later and was photographed tossing paper towels into a crowd. He publicly praised his administration's response as an "incredible, unsung success." He disputed the higher death toll as a political fabrication. He later withheld $20 billion in approved disaster relief.

📁 First Term Record — documented history

Puerto Ricans are American citizens. They pay federal taxes. Their young people serve in the US military at higher rates than many states. When Hurricane Maria hit, 3.3 million American citizens lost power — in many cases for months. The longest power outage in American history followed. Hospitals ran on generators. People with chronic conditions — dialysis patients, those requiring refrigerated medications — died. The initial death toll of 64 was derived from death certificate data; the George Washington University study commissioned by Puerto Rico's governor later analyzed excess mortality — comparing death rates before and after the storm — and found approximately 2,975 additional deaths attributable to the hurricane and its aftermath.

Trump visited Puerto Rico on October 3, 2017 — 13 days after landfall. At a relief distribution center in Guaynabo, he tossed paper towels from behind a table into a small crowd of people who had come to meet him. The images were seen around the world. Trump told the gathered crowd that the death toll — then officially reported as 16 — was something to be "very proud of" compared to "a real catastrophe like Katrina." He called it a "great" response. At the same event he told Puerto Rico's mayor, who had called for more federal help, that she had been "not nice" to him. He complained that Puerto Rico had thrown the federal budget "a little out of whack."

In August 2019, Trump blocked the release of $20 billion in congressionally approved disaster relief for Puerto Rico, citing concerns about corruption. The hold came as Puerto Rico was still rebuilding two years after the storm. Numerous investigations found no basis for Trump's corruption claims regarding the aid. Congressional Democrats and some Republicans condemned the hold. Eventually, after extensive congressional pressure, the funds were released — but the delay caused additional harm to reconstruction efforts still underway.
