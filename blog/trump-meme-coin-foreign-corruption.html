May 22, 2025 corruption emoluments fact-checked

73% of the People Who Bought Dinner With Trump Were Foreign Nationals.

Days before his inauguration, Trump launched a personal cryptocurrency. Then offered private dinner access to the top 220 investors. 73% were likely foreign nationals — including a Chinese billionaire under active SEC fraud investigation, whose case was paused after he invested $45 million in Trump's other crypto venture. Anthony Scaramucci, Trump's own former aide, called it "Idi Amin level corruption." Tucker Carlson called it "corruption." The former Republican chair of the House Ethics Committee called it "completely out of bounds." Nobody did anything about it.

On January 17, 2025 — three days before his inauguration — Donald Trump launched a personal cryptocurrency called $TRUMP. It peaked at $75 a coin, then crashed, wiping out roughly $2 billion in value for small investors. About 764,000 people who bought it lost money. Fifty-eight wallets made over $10 million each. The coin generated over $320 million in transaction fees — almost entirely for Trump's family business. Then in April, the official $TRUMP website announced that the top 220 investors would be invited to a private gala dinner with the president at his golf club near Washington, D.C. The coin surged 60% on the announcement as people raced to buy enough to get a seat. Average cost of a seat: over $1 million.

The Top Holder Is Under Federal Fraud Investigation. Was.

The top holder of $TRUMP coin as of the gala contest was Justin Sun — a Chinese billionaire who has been facing a civil fraud lawsuit from the Securities and Exchange Commission since 2023, alleging market manipulation and unregistered asset sales. In February 2025, shortly after Trump took office and shortly after Sun began investing tens of millions in Trump's crypto ventures, the SEC under Trump paused that litigation. Sun then invested an additional $45 million in Trump's "World Liberty Financial" crypto project — bringing his total investment across Trump crypto ventures to over $75 million. He held $18.6 million in $TRUMP coin and won a seat at the dinner. In what world is this not a bribe? In this one, apparently, because there is no longer any meaningful enforcement mechanism pointed at the president.

A Houston-based shipping company bought $20 million in $TRUMP coin and publicly stated the reason: to "advocate for fair, balanced, and free trade between Mexico and U.S." They said the quiet part out loud. They paid for policy access and told you they did it.

What the Constitution Says About This.

The foreign emoluments clause of the Constitution bars the president from accepting gifts or payments from foreign governments or nationals without congressional consent. US law also prohibits foreign persons from contributing to US political campaigns. The $TRUMP coin operates in the pseudonymous world of blockchain — meaning foreign governments, foreign oligarchs, anyone with money can funnel cash directly to the president without disclosing their identity. Rep. Jamie Raskin's analysis found that 161 of the 220 top buyers — 73% — were likely foreign nationals, purchasing through offshore crypto exchanges that explicitly prohibit US customers. Bloomberg found that 19 of the top 25 holders were likely foreign nationals. Senators Schiff and Warren demanded an ethics investigation. Rep. Raskin opened a formal inquiry. Thirty-five House Democrats demanded a DOJ investigation. The DOJ did not investigate. The Office of Government Ethics did not respond.

The People Calling It Corruption Aren't All Democrats.

This is worth lingering on. Charles Dent — former Republican congressman and former chair of the House Ethics Committee — said foreign entities using the coin to curry favor with the president is "completely out of bounds and raises all sorts of ethical, legal and constitutional issues that must be addressed." Anthony Scaramucci, Trump's own former White House Communications Director, called $TRUMP "Idi Amin level corruption." Tucker Carlson, watching Trump's Middle East maneuvering coincide with large purchases of his coin, said on camera: "Seems like corruption." Republican Senator Lisa Murkowski said she didn't think "it would be appropriate" to charge people for access to the president. Republican Senator Cynthia Lummis, a staunch Trump supporter, said the dinner "gives me pause." Ethereum co-founder Vitalik Buterin said politician-backed coins are "vehicles for unlimited political bribery, including from foreign nation states." When Tucker Carlson and Vitalik Buterin agree it's corruption, you are well past the threshold.
