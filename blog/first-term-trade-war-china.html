First Term Record — 2018–2020 trade war China farmers fact-checked

Trump's First-Term Trade War with China Cost US Farmers $27 Billion in Lost Exports. He Paid Them $28 Billion in Bailouts.

In 2018, Trump launched a trade war with China, imposing tariffs on hundreds of billions in Chinese goods. China responded with retaliatory tariffs that specifically targeted American agricultural products — soybeans, corn, pork, beef. US farm exports to China collapsed. The USDA paid out $28 billion in federal subsidies to compensate farmers — more than the federal government paid in the 2008 automobile industry bailout. The US trade deficit with China, which Trump said he would eliminate, actually increased during the trade war. The Phase One deal, signed in January 2020, required China to buy specific amounts of US goods. China didn't meet the commitments.

Trump had run on a promise to fix what he described as China's unfair trade practices — intellectual property theft, forced technology transfer, state subsidies for Chinese companies. These are legitimate trade concerns that have been raised by administrations of both parties for decades. The specific strategy Trump chose — imposing broad tariffs unilaterally without coordination with allies — was a maximally disruptive approach that economists broadly questioned. The theory was that tariffs would pressure China to negotiate. China's response was to impose retaliatory tariffs on US goods, with particular precision in targeting American agricultural exports from states that had voted for Trump.

China had been the largest buyer of US agricultural goods — particularly soybeans, of which China buys roughly two-thirds of US exports. When the retaliatory tariffs hit, Chinese buyers shifted to Brazil and Argentina. US soybean prices fell sharply. Hog farmers, whose pork was targeted, faced similar collapses in export revenue. The USDA, under pressure from farm-state senators, launched the Market Facilitation Program — paying farmers direct subsidies to compensate for the lost export income. In 2018, the program paid out $8.5 billion. In 2019, it paid $14.5 billion more. Total: approximately $23 billion in direct payments, with additional market stabilization funds pushing the total past $28 billion. The Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis documented that the farm bailout payments exceeded the 2008 auto industry bailout, which totaled approximately $17.4 billion in TARP funds.

The Phase One trade deal, announced with great fanfare in January 2020, required China to purchase $200 billion in US goods above 2017 levels over two years. The Peterson Institute for International Economics tracked Chinese purchases and found China bought roughly 58% of its committed targets in 2020 and about 68% in 2021. Economists generally assessed Phase One as failing to achieve its stated goals — China had not made the structural changes to its economy that the US demanded, and the purchase commitments were not met. The tariffs Trump imposed remain in place today, and consumers pay them through higher prices on imported goods — a tax that the administration collected but largely directed to farmer bailouts rather than to reducing the deficit.
