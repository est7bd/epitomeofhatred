First Term Record — June 2019 immigration children detention fact-checked

The Photographs Were Called "Kids in Cages." The Inspector General Called the Conditions "Dangerous and Overcrowded." Seven Children Died.

In June 2019, the Associated Press reported that a US Border Patrol station in Clint, Texas was holding more than 300 children — some as young as infants — in conditions described by lawyers who visited as filthy, overcrowded, and medically inadequate. Children were sleeping on concrete floors. Some had been there for weeks. Seven children died in US federal immigration custody between 2018 and 2019. The Department of Homeland Security Inspector General visited multiple facilities and called conditions "dangerous and overcrowded" — a "ticking time bomb." This was documented by the government's own investigators.

The photographs from Border Patrol facilities in 2019 showed children in wire-mesh enclosures — which is what gave rise to the "kids in cages" description. The administration pushed back on the phrase, arguing the enclosures were not cages. Whatever one calls them, children were being held in wire-mesh compartments, sleeping on concrete floors with foil blankets, in facilities far over their stated capacity. Lawyers who visited the Clint, Texas facility reported children as young as 7 and 8 years old caring for toddlers, children sick with flu, children who hadn't bathed in weeks, and children without adequate food, water, or supervision.

"We are concerned that overcrowding and prolonged detention represent an immediate risk to the health and safety of DHS agents and officers, as well as those in CBP custody."

— Department of Homeland Security Office of Inspector General, report released July 2, 2019

The DHS Inspector General visited five Border Patrol facilities in Texas in June 2019 and found: at one facility, single adults were held for an average of 71 days — far exceeding the 72-hour limit. At another, 900 people were held in a facility designed for 125. The IG described the situation as "a ticking time bomb." Between 2018 and 2019, seven children died in US federal immigration custody: Jakelin Caal Maquin, age 7; Felipe Gómez Alonzo, age 8; Juan de León Gutiérrez, age 16; Wilmer Josué Ramírez Vásquez, age 2; Carlos Gregorio Hernandez Vasquez, age 16; Darlyn Cristabel Cordova-Valle, age 10; and an unnamed 16-year-old boy. Most died of treatable illnesses — flu, infection — that went undetected or untreated in understaffed facilities.
