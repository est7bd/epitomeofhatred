The 2017 Tax Cuts Added $1.9 Trillion to the National Debt. Corporations Got Permanent Cuts. Yours Expire in 2025.

The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 was the largest tax overhaul in three decades. The corporate tax rate was cut from 35% to 21% — permanently. Individual tax cuts for working Americans were set to expire in 2025. The Congressional Budget Office estimated the law would add $1.9 trillion to the national debt over 10 years. The Tax Policy Center found that by 2027, 83% of the benefits would flow to the top 1%. Trump promised the cuts would "pay for themselves." Every nonpartisan fiscal analysis said otherwise. 📁 First Term Record — documented history

The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act was signed on December 22, 2017. It was the first major legislative achievement of the Trump presidency, passed with zero Democratic votes on party-line margins. Trump and Republican leaders described it as a "middle class miracle" that would grow the economy so fast it would generate enough revenue to offset the cost. None of the nonpartisan scorekeepers agreed. The Congressional Budget Office projected a $1.9 trillion increase in the deficit over ten years. The Joint Committee on Taxation — Congress's own tax analysis body — scored it similarly. The "growth pays for itself" argument was rejected by every independent fiscal analyst who examined the law.

The Structural Inequality Baked Into the Law.

The most revealing feature of the 2017 tax law is not the tax rates themselves — it's the expiration dates. Corporate tax cuts were made permanent. Individual tax cuts for most working Americans were temporary, set to expire in 2025 and cause taxes to rise for most people unless Congress acted to extend them. This wasn't a technical drafting quirk — it was a deliberate political choice. Congress used budget reconciliation rules to pass the bill with only 51 votes, which required that the bill not add to the deficit beyond a 10-year window. To get the corporate cuts to fit under that constraint, the individual cuts were made temporary. The result: a law structured so that corporations got permanent relief while individuals got a sunset that would require future Congresses to act or face political blame for a tax increase.

"This is going to cost me a fortune, this thing, believe me. Believe me, this is not good for me."

— Donald Trump, speaking about the tax bill he was signing. The law's changes to the estate tax and pass-through business income rules benefited the Trump organization directly.

Who Actually Benefited — and Who Was Misled.

The Tax Policy Center analyzed the distributional effects of the law. In 2018, its first year, most taxpayers did see a reduction — but the reductions were heavily skewed upward. By 2027, once the individual cuts expired and only the corporate and top-bracket provisions remained permanent, 83% of the total benefit would flow to the top 1% of earners. The estate tax exemption was nearly doubled, benefiting only estates worth more than $11 million — which represents a tiny fraction of American families. The law also created a 20% deduction for "pass-through" business income, which disproportionately benefited wealthy business owners. The Trump Organization is structured as a collection of pass-through entities.

What It Did to the Deficit.

When Trump took office in January 2017, the annual federal deficit was approximately $585 billion. By fiscal year 2019 — after the tax cuts' full first year in effect — it had risen to $984 billion, almost doubling. The national debt crossed $22 trillion for the first time in February 2019. The promised economic growth sufficient to offset the revenue loss did not materialize at the scale needed. GDP growth during the Trump first term averaged roughly 2.5% — consistent with the Obama-era recovery trajectory, not the 4-6% promised during the tax cut debate. Then COVID hit, and the deficit exploded to $3.1 trillion in fiscal 2020. The structural deficit created by the 2017 cuts was a pre-existing condition that made the pandemic response more fiscally painful than it otherwise would have been.

The Second-Term Extension and Social Security.

The 2025 expiration of the individual cuts created the political pressure for Trump's second-term "One Big Beautiful Bill" — which extended and expanded those cuts while also cutting Medicaid by $900 billion and accelerating the Social Security Trust Fund's path to insolvency. The 2017 bill was designed to force exactly this choice: extend the cuts for individuals or face a tax increase — and use that political pressure to extract further spending cuts to programs that benefit working and low-income Americans. That's the full arc. It started in December 2017 with a tax bill that was sold as a middle-class miracle. Eight years later it's still being paid for by cuts to health care and retirement security.
