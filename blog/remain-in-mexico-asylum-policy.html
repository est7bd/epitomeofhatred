First Term Record — January 2019 immigration asylum cruelty fact-checked

The "Remain in Mexico" Policy Left Asylum Seekers in Cartel Territory While They Waited for US Court Dates. More Than 1,500 Were Murdered, Raped, or Kidnapped.

The Migrant Protection Protocols — commonly called "Remain in Mexico" — required asylum seekers who arrived at the US-Mexico border to wait in Mexico while their US immigration cases were processed, sometimes for months or years. The border cities where they waited — Tijuana, Ciudad Juárez, Matamoros — are among the most dangerous in the world. Human Rights First documented more than 1,500 publicly reported cases of murder, rape, kidnapping, and other violent attacks on people enrolled in the program. They had followed the legal asylum process. They were returned to the people who had tried to kill them.

Seeking asylum is legal under both US law and international treaty. The United States is a signatory to the 1951 Refugee Convention and its 1967 Protocol, which prohibit returning asylum seekers to places where they face persecution — a principle called "non-refoulement." The Migrant Protection Protocols, implemented beginning January 2019, operated in a legal gray zone: asylum seekers were not being returned to their countries of origin, but they were being returned to Mexico — a country they had fled through — and left in border cities where they had no legal status, no housing rights, no work authorization, and no protection from the cartels that controlled large portions of the territory.

Human Rights First, a nonpartisan human rights organization, began tracking publicly reported cases of violence against MPP enrollees in 2019. By 2021, when Biden ended the program, they had documented more than 1,544 publicly reported cases of murder, rape, torture, kidnapping, and other violent attacks on people who were enrolled in Remain in Mexico and waiting for their US court hearings. These are cases that made it into public reporting — the actual number of attacks was certainly higher. Many of the people attacked were the same people who had originally fled to the US because their lives were in danger. The US returned them to places where that danger was immediate.

Children were among those enrolled in the program. Legal representation was nearly impossible — US immigration attorneys could not easily travel to Mexican border cities to prepare cases, and the asylum seekers had no resources to pay for legal help. Studies found that individuals in MPP who had legal representation were far more likely to be granted asylum than those who did not — but under the program's conditions, legal representation was largely a fiction. The program was challenged repeatedly in courts. The Biden administration moved to end it in 2021. The Supreme Court ruled in 2022 that the administration had the authority to end it, but Texas and Missouri's legal challenges created complications. Trump reinstated MPP on his first day back in office in January 2025.
