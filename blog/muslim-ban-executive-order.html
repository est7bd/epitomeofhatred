The Muslim Ban: Trump Signed It on Day 8. Courts Struck It Down Twice. The Third Version Passed. Here's What It Did.

Eight days into his presidency, Trump signed an executive order suspending entry for nationals from seven Muslim-majority countries and halting the US refugee program. Green card holders were stranded at airports. Families were separated. Refugees who had waited years through extensive vetting were turned away. Courts struck it down twice. The third version was upheld 5-4 by the Supreme Court. Trump had called it a "Muslim ban" himself, repeatedly, during the campaign. The administration then spent a year arguing in court it wasn't one.

📁 First Term Record — documented history

On December 7, 2015, Donald Trump called for "a total and complete shutdown of Muslims entering the United States until our country's representatives can figure out what the hell is going on." That press release remained on his campaign website for years. During the campaign, his adviser Rudy Giuliani described being asked to find a "legal" way to implement a Muslim ban. On January 27, 2017 — eight days into the presidency — Trump signed Executive Order 13769, titled "Protecting the Nation from Foreign Terrorist Entry into the United States." It suspended entry from Iran, Iraq, Libya, Somalia, Sudan, Syria, and Yemen — all Muslim-majority countries. It suspended the US refugee admissions program for 120 days. It indefinitely banned Syrian refugees. The administration spent the next 18 months in court arguing this had nothing to do with religion.

"Donald J. Trump is calling for a total and complete shutdown of Muslims entering the United States."

— Trump campaign press release, December 7, 2015, which remained on the campaign website for years

What Happened When It Was Signed.

The executive order was signed on a Friday evening with minimal preparation or inter-agency coordination. Customs and Border Protection officers received no advance guidance. People who had been in the air when the order was signed — including lawful permanent residents with green cards — landed to find themselves detained. Families who had traveled internationally and were returning home were held at airports. Dozens of people with valid visas were stopped from boarding flights abroad. Lawyers flooded international airports with laptops and hastily printed legal briefs. Within 48 hours of the signing, a federal judge in New York issued an emergency stay blocking deportations. Protests erupted at airports across the country.

The Three Bans.

January 27, 2017 — Ban 1

Executive Order 13769. Suspended entry from 7 Muslim-majority countries, halted refugee admissions, indefinitely banned Syrian refugees. A federal judge in Washington state issued a nationwide temporary restraining order February 3. The Ninth Circuit upheld the block February 9. The administration withdrew the order.

March 6, 2017 — Ban 2

Revised executive order removed Iraq from the list and made other modifications to address legal concerns. Courts in Hawaii and Maryland blocked it. The Supreme Court allowed a partial version to go into effect while litigation continued.

September 24, 2017 — Ban 3 (Presidential Proclamation)

Third version replaced the executive orders. Applied to Chad, Iran, Libya, North Korea, Somalia, Syria, Venezuela, and Yemen. Chad was later removed. The Supreme Court upheld this version 5-4 on June 26, 2018 in Trump v. Hawaii. Chief Justice John Roberts wrote for the majority. Sonia Sotomayor's dissent compared the ruling to Korematsu v. United States — the case that upheld Japanese American internment.

January 20, 2021

President Biden revoked the ban on his first day in office. Thousands of people who had been blocked from reuniting with family members, completing immigration processes, or entering the US began restarting their cases — some years after originally being approved.

Who It Actually Affected.

The people most harmed by the ban were not terrorists — the stated rationale for the policy. They were US citizens' spouses, children, and parents from the banned countries who could not travel to America. They were green card holders caught in transit. They were refugees who had survived years of war, displacement, and had cleared the most extensive vetting process the US government runs — often taking 18 months to two years — only to be turned away at the last step. They were doctors, scientists, and students with valid visas who had built lives around being able to come and go. The State Department estimated that in the first year of the ban, more than 180 million people from the affected countries were broadly restricted from obtaining US visas. The administration never presented specific intelligence linking any of the banned countries' nationals to domestic terrorist attacks — because no such intelligence existed to support that specific policy.
