January 6 Was Not a Spontaneous Riot. It Was the Last Step of a Months-Long Plan to Overturn an Election.

The attack on the US Capitol on January 6, 2021 was not a crowd that got out of hand. It was the final act of a coordinated, documented campaign to overturn the results of a presidential election that Donald Trump lost. There were fake electors in seven states. There were memos written by lawyers advising Trump on how to make Pence declare him the winner. There were attempts to install a loyalist at the DOJ who would announce fraud that didn't exist. And there was a president who watched it happen on television and did nothing for hours. This is the documented record.

The House Select Committee to Investigate the January 6th Attack on the United States Capitol spent 18 months gathering evidence, conducting 1,000+ interviews, and reviewing millions of pages of documents. Its final report, released December 2022, is 845 pages. Multiple federal and state prosecutors have since brought indictments corroborating its findings. John Eastman, the lawyer who wrote the memos advising Trump how to overturn the election, was disbarred in California in 2024. Kenneth Chesebro and Sidney Powell pleaded guilty in the Georgia case. What follows is the documented record — not speculation, not partisan framing — of what actually happened and who did it.

The Plan Had Five Main Components.

1. Fake electors. In seven states Biden won — Arizona, Georgia, Michigan, Nevada, New Mexico, Pennsylvania, and Wisconsin — the Trump campaign organized slates of Republicans to meet on December 14, 2020 (the day certified electors cast their votes) and sign documents falsely claiming to be the legitimate electors for those states. Rudy Giuliani coordinated the effort across states. Kenneth Chesebro wrote the legal memos outlining the strategy. These were not competing slates of legitimate electors. Every court challenge had failed. Every state had certified its results. There was no legal basis. Pence's own counsel, Greg Jacob, told the January 6 Committee directly: there were not, in fact, dual slates of electors from seven states. There was one slate from each state — the certified Biden slates.

2. The Eastman memos. John Eastman wrote two memos advising Trump on how January 6 could be used to reverse the election. The first, a two-page document from December 23, 2020, argued that Pence could simply refuse to count Biden's electoral votes from the seven states, leaving Trump with 232 electoral votes to Biden's 222, and that Pence should do this "without asking for permission." The second, longer memo "war-gamed" multiple scenarios. Eastman knew his own plan was legally indefensible. On December 19, 2020 — four days before sending the first memo — Eastman had himself written in an email that the fake electors were "dead on arrival in Congress" because they lacked state certification. He pushed the plan anyway. Conservative appellate judge J. Michael Luttig — who advised Pence not to go along with the scheme — testified to the Committee that Eastman's theory was "incorrect at every turn."

"There was no support whatsoever in either the Constitution of the United States nor the laws of the United States for the Vice President, frankly, ever to count alternative electoral slates from the States that had not been officially certified."

— Judge J. Michael Luttig, conservative jurist who advised Pence, testifying before the House Select Committee, June 2022

3. The DOJ pressure campaign. Trump attempted to install Jeffrey Clark — a DOJ environmental lawyer with no relevant experience — as Acting Attorney General, because Clark had drafted a letter to Georgia and other swing states falsely claiming the DOJ had found significant election fraud. The actual Acting AG, Jeffrey Rosen, refused to send the letter. Trump held an Oval Office meeting on January 3, 2021 in which he nearly fired Rosen and replaced him with Clark. DOJ leadership threatened mass resignation. Trump backed down. The letter was never sent. But the attempt — to weaponize the Justice Department to announce fraud that the DOJ's own investigators had not found — is documented.

4. The Pence pressure campaign. For weeks, Trump and his team pressured Mike Pence to refuse to certify Biden's victory. Trump told the crowd on January 6: "If Mike Pence does the right thing, we win the election." On the night of January 5, Trump called Eastman and asked him to press Pence one more time. Pence's counsel Greg Jacob and the judge Luttig both told Pence the scheme was illegal. Pence certified the election. The crowd that had been told "If Mike Pence does the right thing" then chanted "Hang Mike Pence" while erecting a gallows outside the Capitol.

5. The rally and the march. Trump held a rally on the Ellipse on the morning of January 6. He told the crowd to march to the Capitol. He returned to the White House and watched television coverage of the attack for hours. He did not call the National Guard. He did not call Capitol Police. He did not call Pence to check if he was safe. He did not address the country. His chief of staff, Mark Meadows, was told the situation at the Capitol was out of control. Trump was watching Fox News. Multiple witnesses testified that Trump, upon hearing Secret Service agents at the rally were being attacked by the crowd, said "So what?" Congressional Republicans, Fox News hosts, and his own daughter and son-in-law texted and called asking him to stop it. He did nothing for 187 minutes.

The Timeline.

November 3, 2020

Biden wins the election. Trump refuses to concede. The campaign begins filing lawsuits — 62 in total. They lose 61. The one partial win didn't affect any election outcome.

November–December 2020

Chesebro writes a series of memos outlining the fake elector strategy. Giuliani coordinates across seven states. Fake electors meet in state capitols on December 14 and sign false certificates claiming Trump won their states. The documents are sent to Congress and the National Archives.

December 23, 2020

Eastman sends his two-page memo to Trump's team. Eastman had called Molly Michael, Trump's assistant, at 1:32 PM asking for a "very quick call" with the president. Five minutes later, the White House switchboard called back. The call lasted 23 minutes. Trump was looped in on Eastman's proposal from the start.

January 2, 2021

Trump, Giuliani, and Eastman hold a conference call with roughly 300 Republican state legislators urging them to convene special sessions to replace certified Biden electors with fake Trump electors. The legislators refuse.

January 3, 2021

Trump nearly replaces Acting AG Rosen with Jeffrey Clark. DOJ leadership threatens mass resignation. Trump backs down. The fraudulent Georgia letter is never sent.

January 6, 2021 — 11:17 AM

Trump takes the stage on the Ellipse. He tells the crowd Pence has the power to "send it back to the states." He says "We fight like hell, and if you don't fight like hell, you're not going to have a country anymore." He tells them to march to the Capitol.

January 6, 2021 — 2:44 PM

Rioters breach the Capitol. The joint session of Congress is suspended. Pence is evacuated. The mob reaches the Senate chamber. Some reach Pence's office. They erect a gallows on the Capitol grounds and chant "Hang Mike Pence."

January 6, 2021 — 187 minutes of inaction

Trump watches television in the dining room off the Oval Office. He does not call the National Guard, Capitol Police, or the Pentagon. He tweets attacking Pence at 2:24 PM — as the mob is inside the Capitol. At 4:17 PM, he releases a video telling rioters "We love you, you're very special." At 6:01 PM, he tweets: "These are the things and events that happen when a sacred landslide election victory is so unceremoniously & viciously stripped away."
