They Took 5,569 Children. They Deliberately Didn't Track Them. More Than 1,000 Are Still Not Reunited.

Trump's Zero Tolerance policy separated at least 5,569 children from their parents at the US-Mexico border — including infants — as a deliberate act of cruelty designed to deter migration. The government intentionally built no system to track or reunite families. An ICE official actively worked to prevent reunification. Hundreds of parents were deported without their children. As of November 2023, more than 1,000 children have still not been reunited. The man who designed the policy — Tom Homan — is now Trump's border czar in the second term.

📁 First Term Record — documented history

This is not ancient history. There are children — now older, living in the United States or in Central America — who have not seen their parents since a federal agent separated them at the border. Some were separated in 2017, before the formal "Zero Tolerance" policy was even announced. The US government took at least 5,569 children from their parents between 2017 and 2021. It built no system to track them. When a DHS official quietly started keeping a list so that children could eventually be reunited, his boss told him to stop — because the list made it look like the government was separating families, which the government was publicly denying. This is documented. It happened in the United States of America. The people who designed it are back in power.

The Policy Was Designed to Hurt. That Was the Point.

The Trump administration began separating families at the border as early as June 2017 — nearly a year before Zero Tolerance was officially announced — through a "pilot program" in the El Paso border sector. Adults crossing without authorization, including asylum seekers, were criminally prosecuted. Their children were classified as "unaccompanied minors" — even though they had arrived with their parents — and sent to Office of Refugee Resettlement shelters, often thousands of miles away. By late 2017, separations were happening along the entire border. The formal Zero Tolerance policy was announced in May 2018 and lasted six and a half weeks before public outcry forced Trump to sign an executive order ending it. But separations continued for at least 18 more months after that. The policy's purpose was stated by officials at the time: family separation was meant to deter future migrants. Trump confirmed it in a 2024 interview, saying the policy "stopped people from coming by the hundreds of thousands because when they hear family separation, they say, well, we better not go." He said this as a defense of the policy.

"The idea was to hurt children and families to scare other people away from coming to this country."

— Jacob Soboroff, NBC News correspondent and author of "Separated," describing what administration officials themselves told him

They Deliberately Built No System to Reunite Families.

This is the detail that needs to be understood clearly: the failure to track separated children was not an administrative oversight. It was a choice. The Atlantic's Caitlin Dickerson, after an 18-month investigation, documented that Scott Lloyd — director of the Office of Refugee Resettlement — had directed his staff not to maintain a list of children who had been separated from their parents. When a DHS official named Carlos Ruiz de La Cruz quietly started keeping his own list so that reunification might someday be possible, his boss was furious. He told De La Cruz to stop — because the list made it look like the government was separating families while the administration was publicly denying it. De La Cruz and his colleagues kept adding to the list anyway. Matt Albence, the head of ICE enforcement and removal operations, had told colleagues to prevent reunification even after parents had cleared the justice system — because reunification, in his words, "undermines the entire effort." In 2019, NBC News reported that although the government claimed it had a "central database" to reconnect families, it actually had enough information to reconnect only sixty children with their parents.

The Timeline of What Actually Happened.

March 2017 — 6 weeks after inauguration

Reuters reports the Trump administration is considering separating children from mothers at the border as a deterrence measure. The administration denies it.

Mid-2017 — El Paso pilot program begins

ICE begins separating families in the El Paso sector as a "pilot program." No reunification system is built. Children reclassified as "unaccompanied" even though they arrived with parents.

Late 2017

Separations expand to the entire US-Mexico border. By April 2018, approximately 700 children under age 4 have already been separated. The administration denies having a family separation policy.

May 7, 2018 — Zero Tolerance announced

Attorney General Jeff Sessions announces Zero Tolerance: all adults crossing without authorization will be criminally prosecuted, with no exceptions for parents with children. Sessions cites the Bible — Romans 13 — to justify the policy.

June 2018

2,342 children separated in just the 35-day period May 5 to June 9, 2018. Photos of children in wire-mesh enclosures emerge. Audio of children crying for their parents recorded inside a CBP facility surfaces publicly.

June 20, 2018 — Trump signs executive order

Under intense public pressure, Trump ends family separation by executive order while falsely blaming Congress, courts, and previous administrations. He had claimed weeks earlier he had no power to end it by executive action.

June 2018 — July 2022 (and beyond)

Reunification attempts reveal the full scale of the tracking failure. 37 children forced to wait in a van for up to 39 hours in Texas heat during a 2018 "reunification" attempt. Some separated families turn out to be in different countries. Hundreds of parents had already been deported without their children.

2023 — Still separated

The Biden administration's Family Separation Task Force reports 1,217 children still not reunited. For 68 of the separated parents, the government has no contact information whatsoever. Many are indigenous Guatemalans from remote communities, speaking languages other than Spanish, with limited phone access.

November 2024 — The architect returns

Trump names Tom Homan — widely described as the architect of family separation — as his border czar for the second term. Homan is the official who oversaw enforcement and removal operations when the policy was implemented.

What It Did to the Children.

Researchers and advocates who interviewed separated families documented severe, lasting trauma. Children returned with PTSD. Some were described as formerly cheerful and rambunctious, returning to their parents thin, with fungal infections, dirty teeth, and full of cavities. One parent said: "They did not give her back to me, they gave me another daughter." Children who were eventually reunited were in some cases too frightened to be separated from their parents even briefly — unable to attend school, unable to allow parents to go to work. This was not an unintended side effect. Officials who implemented the policy knew the harm it would cause. Some told investigators they chose not to push back because it wasn't "strategic" to challenge Stephen Miller's influence over the president.
