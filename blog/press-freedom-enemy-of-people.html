Trump Called the Free Press "the Enemy of the People" More Than 150 Times. Here Is What That Means and What It Led To.

Donald Trump used the phrase "enemy of the people" to describe American news organizations more than 150 times during his first term. The phrase has a specific historical lineage — it was deployed by Stalin to designate targets for purging, and by Mao during the Cultural Revolution. In October 2018, a man mailed pipe bombs to CNN and prominent Democratic figures. In June 2018, a man who had targeted the Capital Gazette newspaper after a dispute with its coverage killed five journalists and staff. The Committee to Protect Journalists documented how autocrats globally adopted Trump's rhetoric to justify jailing and killing journalists.

📁 First Term Record — documented history

Trump first used the phrase "the fake news media is the enemy of the American people" on February 17, 2017 — less than a month into his presidency. He would repeat variations of it at rallies, on Twitter, in press conferences, and in formal speeches more than 150 times over four years. The specific phrase "enemy of the people" — not just "enemy of the country" or "bad for America" — is not accidental. It is a term of political art with a specific meaning in authoritarian governance: it designates a group that is to be suppressed, not merely criticized. Stalin used the phrase to purge party members. Mao used it to justify the persecution of millions during the Cultural Revolution. Trump's use of the exact phrase was understood by historians of authoritarianism as significant from the first time he deployed it.

"The FAKE NEWS media (failing NY Times, NBC News, ABC, CBS, CNN) is not my enemy, it is the enemy of the American People!"

— Donald Trump, tweet, February 17, 2017. His first use of the phrase. He would repeat it more than 150 times.

What the Rhetoric Led To — Domestically.

In June 2018, Jarrod Ramos shot and killed five people at the Capital Gazette newspaper in Annapolis, Maryland. Ramos had a grievance against the paper related to its coverage of a criminal case against him — a pre-existing dispute, not purely ideological. But the attack occurred in a climate in which targeting journalists had been normalized at the highest level of government. In October 2018, Cesar Sayoc mailed pipe bombs to CNN's New York offices and to prominent Democratic figures including Barack Obama, Hillary Clinton, George Soros, and others. Sayoc was a fervent Trump supporter whose van was covered in Trump imagery. In his statements, he cited Trump's rhetoric against the press and Democrats. He was sentenced to 20 years in prison.

The Global Effect — Autocrats Adopted the Phrase.

The Committee to Protect Journalists documented one of the most significant effects of Trump's press attacks: authoritarian leaders around the world began using the phrase "fake news" and "enemy of the people" to justify cracking down on their own journalists. CPJ documented leaders in Cambodia, Libya, Syria, Myanmar, and elsewhere explicitly citing Trump's language. Egyptian President Abdel Fattah el-Sisi invoked "fake news" when asked about journalists imprisoned in Egypt. Philippine President Rodrigo Duterte praised Trump's press attacks. The destruction of norms around press freedom in the United States provided cover for regimes worldwide. When the leader of the world's most prominent democracy calls journalists the "enemy of the people," it makes it vastly easier for every authoritarian who jails and kills journalists to claim they're doing the same thing Trump does.
