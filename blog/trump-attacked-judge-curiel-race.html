Trump Said a Federal Judge Couldn't Be Fair Because of His Mexican Heritage.

In June 2016, Donald Trump — then the presumptive Republican presidential nominee — claimed that Gonzalo Curiel, the federal judge overseeing the Trump University fraud lawsuit, could not be impartial because of his "Mexican heritage." Judge Curiel was born in Indiana. He is an American citizen. He was appointed by President Obama. House Speaker Paul Ryan, who had just endorsed Trump, called the remarks "the textbook definition of a racist comment." Trump doubled down and said he stood by everything he said.

The Trump University case was a fraud lawsuit alleging that Trump's for-profit education company made false promises to students about the quality of education they would receive, leading them to pay tens of thousands of dollars for courses that did not deliver what was advertised. The case was pending before Judge Curiel when Trump began his attacks. The attacks were not subtle or ambiguous — Trump said directly and repeatedly that Curiel's background made him inherently biased against Trump because of Trump's stated intention to build a wall on the Mexican border.

What Trump Said.

Trump told the Wall Street Journal: "I'm building a wall. It's an inherent conflict of interest." He told CNN's Jake Tapper that Curiel was "a hater of Donald Trump" and that "he's of Mexican heritage." When Tapper pointed out that Curiel was born in Indiana and was an American, Trump said: "He's of Mexican heritage." When Tapper asked whether a Muslim judge would also be unable to be fair to Trump given his proposed Muslim ban, Trump said: "It's possible, yes. Yeah. That would be possible, absolutely." He said this on live television.

The theory Trump was advancing — that a judge's ethnic background determines their capacity to be impartial — is a direct attack on the foundation of an independent judiciary. If accepted, it would mean that no minority judge could hear any case involving a party who had made statements about that minority group. It is not a legal position. It is not a political position. It is a statement that people of certain backgrounds cannot be trusted to do their jobs because of who they are.

The Republican Response.

Paul Ryan, who had just endorsed Trump days earlier after months of resistance, called the remarks "the textbook definition of a racist comment" — then said he still supported Trump. Mitch McConnell said Trump should "get on script." Newt Gingrich called it "one of the worst mistakes Trump has made." Then he also continued supporting Trump. The Republican Party criticized the comments, condemned the comments, and then supported the candidate who made them.

Trump settled the Trump University lawsuits in November 2016 — after winning the election — for $25 million, without admitting wrongdoing. Judge Curiel approved the settlement. The man Trump accused of being incapable of fairness presided over a resolution that ended the case.

Verification note

Trump's Wall Street Journal and CNN interviews on Judge Curiel are from June 2016 and are on record with full transcripts and video available. Paul Ryan's "textbook definition of a racist comment" quote is from a press conference June 7, 2016, widely reported. The $25 million Trump University settlement was confirmed by the Department of Justice, November 2016. Judge Curiel's Indiana birth and Obama appointment are matters of public court record.
