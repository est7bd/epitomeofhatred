Tomorrow, 9 Million Americans Are Expected to Protest. It Could Be the Largest Single Day of Protest in US History.

Saturday March 28 is the third No Kings Day of Action. More than 3,000 protests in all 50 states. Nine million people signed up. Bruce Springsteen will perform in Minneapolis — named for Renee Good and Alex Pretti, the two Americans ICE killed there. Two-thirds of events are outside major cities. Idaho, Wyoming, Montana, Utah all have protests in the double digits. This is no longer a blue-city march. Researchers say it may be the largest coordinated single-day protest in American history.

The No Kings movement began in June 2025, organized by Indivisible and affiliated groups in response to what organizers describe as the Trump administration's systematic dismantling of democratic norms — mass deportations, ICE killings of American citizens, the war in Iran launched without congressional authorization, the firing of independent agency heads, and the use of the Justice Department as a political weapon. The June protest drew roughly five million participants. October drew seven million. Saturday's third action is expected to break both records. Organizers have signed up more than nine million people across more than 3,000 events in all 50 states and cities around the world. Researchers affiliated with the nonpartisan Brookings Institution and American University, who have been surveying protest participants since the Women's March of 2017, say March 28 may be the single largest day of domestic political protest in American history.

Minneapolis Is the Flagship. For a Reason.

The central event is in Minneapolis — and that is deliberate. Renee Good and Alex Pretti were both killed by ICE in Minneapolis earlier this year. Their deaths are the reason Senate Democrats held firm on refusing to fund ICE without reforms, which triggered the 42-day DHS shutdown, which emptied TSA payrolls, which brought you the airport lines that have dominated the news cycle this week. Every thread of this crisis runs back through Minneapolis. Bruce Springsteen will perform a song called "Streets of Minneapolis" — written for this moment, for this movement, for those two names. The protest organizers have described the Twin Cities event as the movement's "flagship site." It is a statement about where this resistance comes from and who it is for.

"With every ICE raid, every escalation abroad, and every abuse of power at home, Americans are rising up in opposition to Trump's attempt to rule through fear and force." — Ezra Levin, co-Executive Director, Indivisible

This Is Not a Democrat March Anymore.

This is the most important thing about Saturday that mainstream coverage is missing. The researchers from Brookings who have been tracking the No Kings movement since its beginning found a striking demographic shift from June 2025 to October 2025: the share of women protesters fell from 77% to 57%. The movement is attracting more men. It's also expanding geographically in a way that partisan protest movements almost never do. Sixty-six percent of Saturday's planned protests are outside major urban centers. Idaho has protests in the double digits. Wyoming. Montana. Utah. These are events in Republican lawmakers' own backyards, in communities where Trump won by 30 points. "We are seeing in Republican leaders' own backyards powerful pushback," said Leah Greenberg, co-Executive Director of the Indivisible Project. When people in rural Wyoming are taking to the streets against a Republican president, something has changed. That's not a partisan base venting. That's a broader public making a decision.

What They're Protesting — The Full List.

The movement was originally organized around immigration enforcement and the ICE killings in Minneapolis. Since then, the list of grievances that brought 9 million people to sign up has grown substantially: the war in Iran launched without congressional authorization, now in its 27th day with 13 Americans dead; the DHS shutdown that left 61,000 TSA workers without paychecks for 42 days while ICE remained fully funded; the systematic firing of Black and female military officers; the use of the DOJ to prosecute political opponents while dismantling the offices that would investigate the administration itself; the $580 million in oil futures traded minutes before Trump's Iran announcement; the meme coin that sold presidential access to foreign nationals; DOGE cutting 260,000 federal jobs while Musk admitted the effort was "only somewhat successful." The administration has given them a lot to work with. They showed up anyway.
