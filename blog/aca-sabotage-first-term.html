First Term Record — 2017–2021 healthcare ACA sabotage fact-checked

Trump Said "Let Obamacare Explode." Then He Spent Four Years Trying to Make It.

When Congress failed to repeal the Affordable Care Act in July 2017 — stopped by John McCain's famous thumbs-down vote — Trump didn't give up. He turned to administrative sabotage: cutting the subsidies that made coverage affordable, gutting the outreach programs that helped people enroll, eliminating the individual mandate through the tax bill, promoting junk plans that exclude preexisting conditions, and directing his DOJ to argue the entire law was unconstitutional. 3.2 million Americans lost health coverage in year one. All documented from CBO analyses, CBPP research, and Trump's own statements.

📁 First Term Record — documented history

Trump was direct about his strategy. When the legislative repeal failed, he said: "Politically, the best thing to do would be to let the ACA explode." His chief strategist Steve Bannon was even blunter, saying the plan was to "blow those exchanges up." These were not offhand comments — they described what the administration proceeded to do, systematically, for four years.

"Not gonna make the CSR payments, gonna blow that thing up, gonna blow those exchanges up, right?"

— Steve Bannon, former White House Chief Strategist, speaking in October 2017 shortly after Trump cut cost-sharing reduction payments to insurers

The Documented Acts of Sabotage.

January 20, 2017 — Day One

Executive order directing agencies to undermine the ACA "to the maximum extent permitted by law." The first executive action of the Trump presidency. As a direct result, the IRS canceled a new rule that would have tightened enforcement of the individual mandate, sending a signal to insurers that the law's stabilizing mechanism would not be enforced.

January 26, 2017

Enrollment outreach advertising canceled. The Trump administration canceled millions in outreach advertising during the final crucial week of open enrollment — typically the week with the highest sign-up surge. The former chief marketing officer of HealthCare.gov estimated this reduced enrollment by nearly 500,000 people.

Fall 2017

Navigator funding slashed 90%. Navigator programs — which provide in-person assistance helping people understand and sign up for coverage — had their federal funding cut by approximately 90%. These programs particularly served low-income people, older adults, and people with limited English proficiency who most needed help navigating the enrollment process.

Fall 2017

Open enrollment period cut in half. The ACA marketplace open enrollment period was reduced from 90 days to 45 days. The administration also scheduled 12 hours of HealthCare.gov maintenance outages on Sundays during open enrollment — the day with historically the highest traffic. Healthcare.gov also went offline for several hours during the final enrollment push.

October 12, 2017

Cost-sharing reduction payments cut. The administration halted payments to insurers for cost-sharing reductions (CSRs) — the subsidies that reduce deductibles and copays for low-income enrollees. The Congressional Budget Office estimated this would increase premiums by 20% in 2018 and 25% by 2020, result in 1 million more uninsured people, and add nearly $200 billion to the federal deficit over a decade. Insurers in multiple states explicitly cited the CSR cut when raising rates.

December 2017

Individual mandate eliminated — buried in the tax bill. The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act zeroed out the individual mandate penalty starting in 2019. CBO projected this would cause 13 million more people to be uninsured and push premiums up another 10% by driving healthy people out of the market. Insurance companies included mandate elimination as an explicit reason for premium increases in their rate filings.

2018

Junk plans expanded. The Trump administration issued rules allowing insurers to sell "short-term" plans lasting up to a year, renewable for three years — plans completely exempt from ACA requirements. A KFF analysis found that among short-term plans studied: none covered maternity care, 62% didn't cover substance abuse treatment, and 71% didn't cover outpatient prescriptions. These plans could deny people with preexisting conditions. Healthy people who enrolled left the ACA market, raising costs for those who remained.

2020

DOJ argued entire ACA was unconstitutional. Against the advice of his Attorney General, Trump directed the DOJ to argue in court that the entire Affordable Care Act should be struck down — including its protections for the 133 million Americans with preexisting conditions. The Supreme Court ultimately upheld the ACA in 2021.

The Result: 3.2 Million Lost Coverage in Year One.

The Gallup polling organization tracked the national uninsured rate throughout the Trump first term. After years of gains under the ACA, the national uninsured rate rose by 1.3 percentage points in 2017 alone — representing approximately 3.2 million Americans who lost health coverage. This was the first increase in the uninsured rate since the ACA's implementation. Seventeen states experienced statistically significant increases. The CEO of CareFirst Blue Cross Blue Shield said the marketplaces were "materially worse" under the Trump administration due to its "deliberate actions to undermine the system." Premiums for ACA marketplace plans increased substantially — families facing $102 more per month in premiums attributable to the first round of sabotage.
