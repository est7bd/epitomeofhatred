First Term Record — May 8, 2018 Iran nuclear deal JCPOA fact-checked

Trump Withdrew from the Iran Nuclear Deal That Was Preventing Iran from Getting a Bomb. Iran Is Now Closer to One Than Ever.

The Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action — the 2015 nuclear deal with Iran, negotiated by the US, UK, France, Germany, Russia, and China — verifiably capped Iran's uranium enrichment at 3.67%, limited its stockpile, and established the most intrusive nuclear inspection regime in history. The deal pushed Iran's "breakout time" to nuclear capability to 12 months. Trump called it "the worst deal ever" and withdrew in May 2018 with no alternative ready. By 2023, Iran had enriched uranium to 83.7% — one step from weapons grade. By 2025, the breakout time was estimated at weeks. Trump then launched a war with Iran to stop a weapons program his withdrawal accelerated.

The deal was not perfect. Its critics — including many who supported withdrawal — pointed out that it had a 15-year sunset clause, that it didn't address Iran's ballistic missile program, and that it didn't constrain Iran's regional proxy activities. These are legitimate criticisms. The question is: what did withdrawal accomplish? The answer is documented in the IAEA's quarterly reports. Before the deal, Iran had 19,000 centrifuges and was enriching to 20%. The deal got that down to 5,060 centrifuges enriching to 3.67%. After Trump's withdrawal, Iran responded by violating the caps incrementally, then rapidly. Each step was Iran's response to the US tearing up the deal and reimposing sanctions without getting anything in return.

The European allies — UK, France, Germany — stayed in the deal after Trump withdrew and pleaded with the US to return. The Biden administration attempted to rejoin in 2021 but negotiations collapsed before a deal was reached. Iran used the years between Trump's withdrawal and any potential return to dramatically advance its nuclear capabilities. The "maximum pressure" campaign Trump launched produced no concessions from Iran — it produced a more advanced nuclear program and a regime that concluded it was better off enriching uranium than negotiating with an American president who walked away from signed agreements.
