The Second Impeachment: 232 House Members. 57 Senators. All for Incitement of Insurrection. He Was Acquitted Anyway.

Seven days before his term ended, Donald Trump was impeached by the House of Representatives for a second time. The charge: incitement of insurrection. The vote: 232-197, with 10 Republicans crossing over — the most bipartisan impeachment vote against a sitting president in US history. In the Senate trial, 57 senators voted guilty — a majority. But the two-thirds threshold is 67. He was acquitted 57-43. He is the only person in American history to be impeached twice. This is the documented record.

📁 First Term Record — documented history

On January 11, 2021 — five days after the Capitol attack — Representatives Jamie Raskin, David Cicilline, and Ted Lieu introduced a single article of impeachment charging Trump with "incitement of insurrection." The article cited Trump's months of false election fraud claims, his January 2 phone call to Georgia Secretary of State Brad Raffensperger in which he asked him to "find" 11,780 votes, and his speech on January 6 in which he told a crowd to "fight like hell" and then sent them to the Capitol. The House passed the article on January 13, 2021 — the fastest impeachment in US history, seven days after the attack it concerned.

What the Article Said.

The article of impeachment charged Trump with "incitement of insurrection," stating that he had "willfully made statements that encouraged — and foreseeably resulted in — lawless action at the Capitol." It cited his months of false fraud claims, his pressure on state officials to reverse results, and specifically the January 6 rally where he told the crowd that "if you don't fight like hell, you're not going to have a country anymore" before directing them to march to the Capitol. The article noted that his conduct "followed his prior efforts to subvert and obstruct the certification of the results of the 2020 presidential election." It concluded that Trump had "demonstrated that he will remain a threat to national security, democracy, and the Constitution."

"If provoking an insurrectionary riot against a Joint Session of Congress after losing an election is not an impeachable offense, it is hard to imagine what would be."

— House impeachment managers' brief, February 2021

Most of these members faced primary challenges, lost their seats, or were censured by their state Republican parties after their votes. Liz Cheney was stripped of her House leadership position and eventually lost her primary to a Trump-backed challenger. Adam Kinzinger chose not to seek reelection. Peter Meijer lost his primary. The Republican Party made clear: voting to hold Trump accountable for January 6 was a career-ending act.

The Senate Trial and the 57-43 Vote.

The trial ran from February 9-13, 2021. Lead impeachment manager Jamie Raskin — who had attended a family member's funeral on January 6 before going to the Capitol and sheltering under a desk — presented the case. Impeachment managers played a 13-minute video montage of the attack, Trump's rally speech, and the chaos that followed. They introduced evidence that Trump had called Senator Tommy Tuberville during the attack to ask him to delay the certification, and that he had called House Minority Leader Kevin McCarthy during the riot — with McCarthy telling Trump his supporters were "trying to f***ing kill me" and Trump responding that the rioters seemed "more upset about the election than you are." When the vote came, 57 senators voted guilty — 7 Republicans joined all 50 Democrats. It was the largest bipartisan vote to convict a president in US history. It was not enough. Conviction requires 67. He was acquitted 57-43.

What Acquittal Meant — and What It Didn't.

Acquittal in an impeachment trial is not a finding of innocence. It is a finding that a sufficient number of senators — for whatever reason — chose not to convict. Most of the 43 Republicans who voted not guilty did so on the grounds that the Senate lacked jurisdiction to try a former president (Trump had left office on January 20, before the trial began). The Senate voted 56-44 to affirm the trial's constitutionality — meaning the jurisdictional argument failed even to persuade many who ultimately voted to acquit. Senator Richard Burr of North Carolina, who voted to convict on the merits, said: "The evidence is compelling that President Trump is guilty of inciting an insurrection against a coequal branch of government." He voted to convict. Forty-three senators voted not guilty anyway. Trump walked away. Four years later he was back in the White House.
