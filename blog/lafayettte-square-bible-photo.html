First Term Record — June 1, 2020 Lafayette Square protesters abuse of power fact-checked

Police Tear-Gassed Peaceful Protesters So Trump Could Walk to a Church and Hold a Bible for Photos.

On June 1, 2020 — the fifth day of nationwide protests following George Floyd's murder — law enforcement officers used chemical irritants, rubber bullets, and mounted police to forcibly clear Lafayette Square, the park across from the White House, of peaceful protesters who had the legal right to be there. Approximately 30 minutes before the square was cleared, Trump had been moved to the White House bunker as protests grew. After the clearing, Trump walked across the empty square to St. John's Episcopal Church, stood in front of it, and held a Bible up for photographers. He did not enter the church. He did not open the Bible. He did not pray. The action was documented on live television. General Mark Milley was present in battle dress uniform. He later publicly apologized.

The timing of the Lafayette Square clearing was documented to the minute. At 6:30 PM on June 1, law enforcement used chemical irritants — the Park Police initially denied using tear gas, which independent analysis of the substances used contradicted — to push protesters out of the square. At 7:01 PM, Trump appeared in the Rose Garden, gave a speech about being a president of "law and order" and threatening to deploy the military under the Insurrection Act. At 7:20 PM, he walked across the now-cleared square. The protesters had not been violent. The square had been ordered cleared specifically to allow Trump's photo opportunity.

"I should not have been there. My presence in that moment and in that environment created a perception of the military involved in domestic politics."

— General Mark Milley, Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, in a video message to National Defense University graduates, June 11, 2020 — the rare public apology from a sitting Chairman of the Joint Chiefs for participating in a presidential political event

St. John's Episcopal Church — known as the "Church of the Presidents" because every president since James Madison has worshipped there — had sustained minor damage in fires during the previous night's protests. Trump held the Bible up to cameras. When asked by a reporter if it was his Bible, he said "it's a Bible." The Rt. Rev. Mariann Budde, Bishop of the Episcopal Diocese of Washington, said Trump had "turned a Bible, the most sacred text of the Christian tradition, into a political prop." The Episcopal Church had not been notified of his visit and explicitly condemned it. Trump's use of the military's senior leadership as a political backdrop was what prompted Milley's unusual public apology.
