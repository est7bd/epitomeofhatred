The Emoluments Problem: Foreign Governments Paid Trump's Businesses While He Was President. The Constitution Prohibits This.

The Constitution's Emoluments Clauses are explicit: a president cannot receive payments from foreign governments without congressional consent. Trump refused to divest from his businesses. Foreign governments — Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, the UAE, Turkey, China, and others — booked rooms, purchased memberships, held events, and paid fees at Trump-owned properties throughout his first term. The Trump Hotel in Washington DC made $40 million in 2017, its most profitable year, largely attributable to foreign government spending. Congress investigated. Courts heard cases. No accountability followed. The pattern is back in full force in the second term with the meme coin and Trump Gold Card.

📁 First Term Record — documented history

The Foreign Emoluments Clause of the US Constitution (Article I, Section 9) states that no person holding a federal office "shall, without the Consent of the Congress, accept of any present, Emolument, Office, or Title, of any kind whatever, from any King, Prince, or foreign State." The Domestic Emoluments Clause (Article II, Section 1) prohibits a president from receiving any emolument from the United States or any individual state beyond their official salary. These provisions exist specifically to prevent foreign powers from using financial incentives to influence American government officials. Trump entered office with more than 500 business entities in his name, spanning real estate, hotels, golf courses, and licensing deals, and refused to sell them — placing him in potential violation from day one.

Documented Examples of Foreign Government Payments.

Saudi Arabia — Trump International Hotel, Washington DC

A lobbying firm working for Saudi Arabia spent approximately $270,000 at Trump International Hotel in Washington DC in 2018, booking more than 500 nights for Saudi officials and associates. This spending directly benefited Trump's business while Saudi Arabia was seeking favorable US policy positions. The Saudi government also signed a deal with the Trump Organization in 2015 to build a hotel in Jeddah.

Kuwait — Annual National Day celebration

Kuwait moved its annual National Day celebration — a major diplomatic event — from the Four Seasons Hotel to the Trump International Hotel shortly after Trump's election, spending approximately $60,000. Kuwaiti officials confirmed the change was made to accommodate the new administration's expectations.

Mar-a-Lago memberships — foreign nationals and governments

Mar-a-Lago membership fees doubled from $100,000 to $200,000 after Trump's election. The club became a de facto second White House, with meetings between foreign leaders and Trump regularly occurring there. Documents released under FOIA showed that at least 52 members were foreign nationals — raising concerns about who had access to the president and at what price.

Trump Hotel DC — overall foreign government revenue

The Trump International Hotel in Washington DC reported revenues of approximately $40 million in 2017, its most profitable year, according to documents the Trump Organization provided to DC officials. The hotel's clientele shifted markedly toward foreign government delegations and lobbyists after the inauguration. A House Oversight Committee investigation found foreign governments spent millions at Trump properties during his presidency.

China — Trump trademarks

China approved 38 Trump trademarks — many that had been pending for years — in 2017 and 2018, roughly coinciding with periods of US-China trade negotiations. The Chinese government granting valuable intellectual property rights to a sitting president during negotiations with that president's administration presents an obvious conflict of interest whether or not money changed hands directly.

Why No Accountability.

Multiple lawsuits were filed challenging Trump's emoluments violations — by Democratic members of Congress, by the attorneys general of Maryland and DC, and by CREW (Citizens for Responsibility and Ethics in Washington). The courts ultimately dismissed the cases, largely on standing grounds — ruling that the plaintiffs didn't have the right to sue rather than ruling on the constitutional merits. The Supreme Court vacated the lower court rulings in 2021 after Trump left office, rendering them moot. Congress investigated but took no action beyond oversight hearings. The House Oversight Committee obtained Trump Organization financial documents showing the scope of foreign government spending, but the Republican Senate refused to act. The constitutional provisions that the Founders specifically designed to prevent foreign corruption of American presidents were tested and found to have no practical enforcement mechanism when a president refuses to comply and the opposing party controls only one chamber.

The Second-Term Pattern.

The emoluments concern has not ended — it has expanded. In the second term, Trump launched a meme coin that raised $320 million in fees, with 73% of purchases coming from foreign nationals and entities. The Trump Gold Card visa program charges $5 million for a special residency visa. Foreign nationals purchasing from a sitting president to gain access or favor is the exact scenario the Emoluments Clause was written to prevent. The scale is larger. The mechanism is more direct. The accountability is still absent.
