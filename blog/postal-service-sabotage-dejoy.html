First Term Record — August 2020 USPS election sabotage fact-checked

They Removed Sorting Machines and Mailboxes Before the 2020 Election. The Postmaster General Was a Trump Mega-Donor.

In June 2020, the Trump administration installed Louis DeJoy — a logistics executive and Trump mega-donor with no postal experience — as Postmaster General. Within weeks, DeJoy ordered changes that immediately slowed mail delivery: eliminating overtime, removing mail sorting machines from facilities, removing blue mailboxes from streets, and instructing carriers to leave mail behind rather than make extra trips. This occurred as a pandemic made mail-in voting more critical than ever, and as Trump was publicly saying mail-in voting would lead to fraud. The timing was not coincidental.

📁 First Term Record — documented history

The United States Postal Service is a constitutional institution — Article I, Section 8 authorizes Congress to establish post offices and post roads. It processes approximately 430 million pieces of mail per day. In 2020, amid the COVID-19 pandemic, tens of millions of Americans planned to vote by mail — a practice endorsed by election officials of both parties for decades. Trump spent the summer of 2020 claiming, falsely, that mail-in voting was rife with fraud. At the same time, his newly installed Postmaster General was implementing changes that would make mail slower.

"They want three and a half billion dollars for the mail-in votes, universal mail-in ballots. They want twenty-five billion dollars, billion, for the Post Office. Now, they need that money in order to have the Post Office work so it can take all of these millions and millions of ballots."

— Donald Trump, Fox Business interview, August 13, 2020, explicitly explaining why he would block postal funding

The changes DeJoy implemented beginning in mid-July 2020 were stark. Mail sorting machines — which process hundreds of thousands of pieces of mail per hour — were removed from facilities and in some cases destroyed, their parts discarded. The USPS Inspector General later confirmed that 711 mail processing machines were removed in 2020. Overtime that had allowed mail carriers to complete their routes was eliminated. Carriers were instructed to leave mail behind if it would require additional trips. Blue collection mailboxes disappeared from streets in multiple cities. On-time mail delivery rates fell sharply.

Congress held emergency hearings in late August 2020. DeJoy testified and paused some of the changes — he said he would stop removing additional sorting machines before the election. He did not restore the machines already removed or destroyed. Multiple lawsuits were filed. Federal courts in multiple states ordered the Postal Service to prioritize election mail and extend hours on Election Day to process ballots. Despite the disruption, the 2020 election produced record turnout and mail-in ballots were successfully processed in most jurisdictions — in part because election officials and courts took extraordinary measures to compensate for the postal slowdowns.
