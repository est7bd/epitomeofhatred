Trump Fired FBI Director James Comey During the Russia Investigation.

He removed the man overseeing the investigation into his own campaign and then acted shocked that people noticed the conflict.

← all posts
📁 First Term Record — documented history

Trump fired FBI Director James Comey on May 9, 2017, while the bureau was actively investigating Russian election interference and possible links between Russia and Trump campaign figures. The White House initially tried to package the decision as a matter of DOJ recommendations and Comey’s handling of the Clinton email case. That story did not last long. Within days Trump told NBC’s Lester Holt that “this Russia thing” was on his mind when he made the call.

Why this was explosive

The president had just removed the official leading a federal investigation that touched his own campaign and presidency. Even if every formal firing power still existed on paper, the surrounding facts made the move look like raw interference.

The deeper problem was not just the firing itself. It was the context. Comey had recently confirmed to Congress that the Russia investigation was ongoing. Senior administration officials were already under scrutiny. And the White House response kept shifting in ways that made the official explanation look less believable every time someone new opened their mouth.

The Holt Interview Blew Up the Cleanup Story.

Once Trump publicly said Russia was part of his thinking, the effort to sell the firing as routine management basically collapsed. What had been framed as a clean personnel decision suddenly looked like exactly what critics feared: a president using his authority against the head of an agency conducting an investigation he hated.

That is why the Comey firing still matters. It was one of the earliest moments where Trump showed, in public, that he viewed investigative independence as a personal insult rather than a constitutional necessity.

It Helped Trigger the Special Counsel Phase.

The fallout from the firing was so severe that it accelerated the next stage of the Russia inquiry. Robert Mueller was appointed special counsel just over a week later. So the move that was supposed to reduce pressure wound up generating even more scrutiny. That pattern would repeat throughout the first term: Trump treated institutions like obstacles, hit them in plain sight, and then acted aggrieved when the backlash got bigger.

The Comey episode was not a side scandal. It was an early road sign for the whole presidency.
