30,573 Lies in Four Years. The Washington Post Counted Every One.

The Washington Post Fact Checker project tracked every false or misleading claim made by Donald Trump from his inauguration on January 20, 2017 through the end of his first term. The total: 30,573 false or misleading statements in 1,461 days — an average of 20.9 per day. In his final year in office, the pace accelerated to 39 false or misleading claims per day. The most repeated claim, told more than 200 times: that Mexico would pay for the wall. This is the documented record. No other president has been fact-checked at this scale because no other president has required it at this scale.

📁 First Term Record — documented history

The Washington Post's fact-checking operation, led by Glenn Kessler, began tracking Trump's statements on the day he took office. The project assigned claims Pinocchios on a four-point scale — from one (some stretching of the facts) to four (a "whopper"). The database grew to tens of thousands of entries, each with a source citation, the original claim, and the fact-checker's assessment. The volume was unprecedented in the project's history. Previous presidents had occasionally made verifiably false statements; Trump did so with a frequency, brazenness, and repetition that required a qualitatively different kind of tracking.

The Most Repeated Claims.

The most repeated false claim: that Mexico would pay for the wall — stated in some form more than 200 times. Mexico did not pay for the wall. The US taxpayer paid, in part. About 450 miles of fencing was built, most of it replacing existing barriers. The second most repeated category: false claims about trade deficits, including that the US had never won a trade case at the World Trade Organization (it had won many), and various inflated or fabricated numbers about trade gaps. The third major category: false claims about the size of the crowd at his inauguration, which he repeated for years despite photographic evidence to the contrary.

Why Volume Matters.

The sheer volume of false claims was not random — it served a strategic purpose that researchers of authoritarian communication have described as "flooding the zone." When a public figure makes false claims at such high frequency, several effects emerge: fact-checkers cannot keep pace and cannot fact-check every claim before it has been absorbed by audiences; the emotional and cognitive effort required to process and challenge each claim exhausts news audiences and political opponents; and the cumulative effect normalizes dishonesty as a feature of political life rather than an aberration. This is distinct from ordinary political spin. The 30,573 figure represents an attempt, documented by a major newspaper over four years, to track the scale of what was being done to American political discourse.
