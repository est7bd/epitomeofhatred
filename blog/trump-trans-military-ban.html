Trump Banned Transgender People from Military Service by Tweet. His Own Military Leaders Hadn't Been Told.

On the morning of July 26, 2017, Donald Trump sent three tweets announcing that the United States would no longer allow transgender individuals to serve in the military "in any capacity." This was a major policy change with legal implications for thousands of currently serving military members. The Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, General Joseph Dunford, had not been notified in advance. Within hours, Dunford issued a memo telling the military to "continue to treat all of our personnel with respect" and maintain current policy until formal guidance was received. Courts blocked the ban. A revised version was eventually upheld by the Supreme Court 5-4. Biden reversed it on Day One. Trump reinstated it on Day One of his second term.

"After consultation with my Generals and military experts, please be advised that the United States Government will not accept or allow Transgender individuals to serve in any capacity in the U.S. Military. Our military must be focused on decisive and overwhelming victory and cannot be burdened with the tremendous medical costs and disruption that transgender in the military would entail. Thank you."

— Donald Trump, three tweets, July 26, 2017. His generals had not been consulted — the Chairman of the Joint Chiefs told the military to wait for formal guidance.

The logistical absurdity of announcing a major military policy via Twitter — without briefing or notifying the civilian and military leadership responsible for implementing it — was documented in real time. Dunford's response memo, sent the same day, was effectively the Joint Chiefs pushing back against a presidential Twitter announcement that had bypassed normal military decision-making processes. The Pentagon didn't know what the policy meant in practice, who it applied to, or how to implement it. Approximately 15,500 transgender individuals were serving in the US military when the tweets were sent.

Trump's stated justification — "tremendous medical costs" — was contradicted by a RAND Corporation study commissioned by the Pentagon under Obama, which found that the costs of transgender military healthcare would be minimal: between $2.4 million and $8.4 million per year — a rounding error in a $700 billion defense budget. The actual motivation, as framing from the White House and congressional allies made clear, was a cultural and political one, not a military readiness argument. The argument that transgender service members created "disruption" was the same argument made against racial integration of the military in 1948 and against allowing women to serve in combat roles. All three have proven unfounded.
