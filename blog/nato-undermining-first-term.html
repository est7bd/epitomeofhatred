Trump Spent His First Term Undermining NATO. Then He Came Back and Said He'd Let Russia Attack Members Who Don't Pay Up.

NATO is a mutual defense alliance where an attack on one member is treated as an attack on all — Article 5. It has been invoked exactly once in its history: after September 11, 2001, when 29 nations declared their collective defense of the United States. Trump questioned the value of NATO from his first campaign through his last day in office, refused to explicitly commit to Article 5 at his first NATO summit, discussed withdrawing the US from the alliance entirely, and then in his 2024 campaign said he would "encourage" Russia to "do whatever the hell they want" to NATO members who hadn't met defense spending targets. The damage was not theoretical.

📁 First Term Record — documented history

At Trump's first NATO summit in Brussels in May 2017, every other NATO leader committed publicly to Article 5 — the collective defense clause. Trump did not. The omission was noticed globally and required a scramble by aides who insisted it hadn't been deliberate. Trump finally said he was "committing" to Article 5 in an offhand remark at a later point, but the damage to alliance confidence was already done. Over the course of the first term, Trump repeatedly demanded that NATO allies increase their defense spending — a legitimate goal, pursued by every president since Eisenhower — but framed it as a protection-for-payment transaction, suggesting that the US might not defend countries that hadn't paid "their bills."

"One of the presidents of a big country stood up and said, 'Well, sir, if we don't pay and Russia attacks us, will you protect us?' I said, 'You didn't pay? You're delinquent?' He said, 'Yes, let's say that happened.' No, I would not protect you. In fact, I would encourage them to do whatever the hell they want. You gotta pay."

— Donald Trump, campaign rally, February 10, 2024

Senior officials in the first Trump administration — including National Security Advisor John Bolton and Defense Secretary James Mattis — have publicly stated that Trump repeatedly expressed interest in withdrawing the United States from NATO entirely. Bolton wrote in his memoir that Trump raised the issue in multiple meetings. Mattis resigned in December 2018 partly over Trump's alliance-undermining behavior. The New York Times reported that Trump discussed withdrawing from NATO on at least three separate occasions in 2018. In each instance, senior officials talked him out of it. The near-misses were not public at the time.

The practical effect was that European allies who had relied on American commitment to collective defense had to plan for the possibility that it no longer existed. Germany, France, and other NATO members began discussing European defense autonomy — building military capacity independent of the US guarantee — in a way they had not seriously considered since the Cold War. The damage to the credibility of the alliance was measured not just in words but in billions of euros redirected toward a hedge against American unreliability. The 2024 campaign statement about encouraging Russia to attack non-paying members was not an offhand comment — it was the explicit articulation of a worldview that had driven four years of first-term behavior.
