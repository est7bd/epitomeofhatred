Trump Knew COVID Was Deadly in February. He Told Woodward. He Lied to Everyone Else.

On February 7, 2020, Donald Trump told journalist Bob Woodward on a recorded phone call that COVID-19 was "deadly stuff" — more deadly than the flu, airborne, and to be taken seriously. For the next several months, Trump told the public the virus would "disappear like a miracle," called it "the new hoax," and compared it to seasonal influenza. He told Woodward he deliberately downplayed it to avoid "panic." More than 400,000 Americans died before he left office.

The recordings are not in dispute. Trump agreed to the interviews. Woodward published them in his book "Rage" in September 2020, with audio. Trump's own voice tells Woodward on February 7: "It's also more deadly than your — you know, your, even your strenuous flus." And: "This is deadly stuff." This was said 17 days before the first confirmed community transmission in the United States.

What He Said in Public.

February 26, 2020 — Trump said the US had 15 cases and "within a couple of days is going to be down to close to zero." February 28 — called Democratic criticism of his COVID response "their new hoax." March 10 — "just stay calm. It will go away." March 30 — "it's going to go away, hopefully at the end of the month." April 7 — "it's going to go away without a vaccine." August 5 — "it's going to disappear, I'll say it again." He said variations of "it will disappear" or "go away" more than 38 times publicly between February and October 2020, according to analysis by the Washington Post.

What He Told Woodward.

On March 19, Trump told Woodward directly: "I wanted to always play it down. I still like playing it down, because I don't want to create a panic." This is Trump, in his own words, on tape, explaining that he was deliberately misrepresenting the danger of a pandemic to the American public — a public that was making decisions about whether to go to work, send children to school, and see elderly relatives based on what their president told them.

Verification note

All quotes are from recorded interviews published in Bob Woodward's "Rage" (September 2020) with accompanying audio. Trump's public statements are from White House press briefings and public events, available in transcript and video. The 38-times figure is from Washington Post analysis published October 2020.

Trump's defense was that he didn't want to cause panic — which is itself an admission that he withheld material public health information from the people he was supposed to protect. When the recordings were published, Trump said Woodward should have reported the information immediately if it was so dangerous. Woodward noted that Trump had the entire federal public health apparatus at his disposal and chose not to use it to warn the public.
