First Term Record — October 2, 2018 Saudi Arabia Khashoggi press freedom fact-checked

The CIA Said Saudi Crown Prince Mohammed Bin Salman Ordered the Murder of Jamal Khashoggi. Trump Said "Maybe He Did, Maybe He Didn't."

On October 2, 2018, Jamal Khashoggi — a Washington Post journalist and US resident — entered the Saudi consulate in Istanbul to obtain documents for his upcoming wedding. He was murdered inside. A team of Saudi operatives, some with ties to the crown prince's personal security detail, dismembered his body. The CIA assessed with high confidence that Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman personally approved the operation. Trump's response was a statement arguing that Saudi oil and arms deals were more important than accountability for the murder of a journalist.

📁 First Term Record — documented history

Jamal Khashoggi had been a prominent Saudi journalist, government insider, and adviser who became critical of Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman's policies and went into self-imposed exile in the United States. He became a contributing columnist for the Washington Post. His columns critical of MBS and Saudi policy had made him a target. On October 2, 2018, Turkish intelligence monitored the Saudi consulate in Istanbul as a 15-member Saudi team — including forensic specialists and members of MBS's personal security detail — murdered and dismembered Khashoggi inside the building. Turkey released audio recordings of the killing. His body has never been found.

"Maybe he did and maybe he didn't! We may never know all of the facts surrounding the murder of Mr. Jamal Khashoggi. In any case, our relationship is with the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia."

— Donald Trump, statement released November 20, 2018, after the CIA concluded with high confidence that MBS ordered the killing

What the CIA Found.

The CIA assessed with "high confidence" that Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman personally approved the killing of Khashoggi. The assessment was based on MBS's control over the operatives involved, communications intercepts, and the nature of the operation. The assessment was classified by the Trump administration and not publicly released until the Biden administration declassified it in February 2021. The declassified report states: "We assess that Saudi Arabia's Crown Prince Muhammad bin Salman approved the operation in Istanbul, Turkey to capture or kill Saudi journalist Jamal Khashoggi." The Trump administration had actively concealed this assessment.

Trump's Defense of MBS.

Trump's November 20, 2018 statement was extraordinary. He acknowledged the CIA's assessment but said the US "may never know all of the facts," argued that Saudi Arabia was "a very important ally" against Iran, emphasized a purported $450 billion in Saudi investment and arms purchases, and concluded: "It is what it is!" He listed economic and strategic reasons why the relationship with Saudi Arabia should not be jeopardized — essentially arguing that $450 billion was the price at which a journalist's life could be covered. The stated arms figure was also dramatically inflated — the actual confirmed deals were a fraction of Trump's claimed number. Congress was furious. The Senate passed a resolution declaring MBS responsible for Khashoggi's death by 56-41. Trump threatened to veto any legislation restricting arms sales to Saudi Arabia.
