Trump Pulled Out of Paris. Then Rolled Back Over 100 Environmental Protections. Here Is the List.

The Trump first term was the most destructive four-year period for US environmental policy in modern history. He withdrew from the Paris Climate Agreement. He reversed or weakened more than 100 federal environmental rules tracked by the New York Times and Columbia Law School's Sabin Center. He gutted the EPA's scientific advisory committees, installing industry representatives. He opened national monuments and protected lands to drilling and mining. He weakened clean water rules protecting wetlands and streams that supply drinking water to millions. This is the documented record.

📁 First Term Record — documented history

In June 2017, Trump announced US withdrawal from the Paris Agreement — the international accord in which 195 nations committed to reducing greenhouse gas emissions to limit global warming. He called it a bad deal that hurt American workers and competitiveness. He said he could negotiate a better one. No renegotiation followed. The US formally withdrew on November 4, 2020 — the day after the 2020 election. Biden rejoined on January 20, 2021, his first day in office. Trump's second term began with immediate re-withdrawal. Beyond Paris, the first-term EPA rollback campaign was systematic and comprehensive — covering air quality, water quality, public lands, climate, and chemical safety.

Major Rollbacks — Documented.

Climate / Air

Clean Power Plan replaced. Obama's signature rule limiting carbon emissions from power plants was replaced with the far weaker "Affordable Clean Energy" rule. Courts later struck the replacement down.

Methane

Oil and gas methane rules reversed. Regulations requiring oil and gas companies to monitor and repair methane leaks were rolled back. Methane is 80x more potent than CO2 over 20 years.

Clean Water

Waters of the United States rule repealed. The rule extended Clean Water Act protections to wetlands, streams, and smaller waterways supplying drinking water to millions. Trump eliminated it.

Fuel Efficiency

Vehicle fuel economy standards weakened. Obama-era standards would have required 54.5 MPG by 2025. Trump froze them at 2020 levels — projected to add 867 million tons of CO2 over the life of vehicles produced under the looser rules.

Public Lands

Bears Ears and Grand Staircase-Escalante shrunk. Trump reduced Bears Ears National Monument by 85% and Grand Staircase-Escalante by nearly half — the largest reduction of protected land in US history — opening them to mining and drilling.

Coal

Coal plant emission limits reversed. The EPA weakened mercury and air toxics standards for coal power plants, a rule that the agency's own analysis said would save lives by reducing toxic air pollution.

Science

EPA science advisory committees gutted. Trump removed scientists from EPA advisory boards and replaced them with industry representatives. He banned scientists receiving EPA grants from serving on those panels.

Offshore Drilling

Offshore drilling safety rules weakened. Post-Deepwater Horizon safety requirements for offshore oil wells — adopted after the 2010 disaster that killed 11 and spilled 134 million gallons — were weakened.

Arctic

Arctic National Wildlife Refuge opened to drilling. ANWR — protected for decades — was opened to oil exploration and drilling in the TCJA's tax bill, using the reconciliation process to avoid a filibuster.

Chemicals

Pesticide restrictions reversed. An Obama-era ban on chlorpyrifos — a pesticide linked to brain damage in children — was reversed. Courts later ordered it reinstated.

The Human Cost Estimate.

The rollbacks weren't just regulatory paperwork. The Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health estimated that the Trump administration's environmental deregulation contributed to increases in particulate air pollution that were associated with approximately 9,700 additional deaths per year. The weakening of vehicle fuel efficiency standards alone was projected by the EPA's own career staff to add hundreds of millions of tons of greenhouse gas emissions. The elimination of methane oversight removed regulations designed to prevent one of the fastest-acting climate pollutants from leaking uncontrolled from thousands of oil and gas operations nationwide. Environmental regulation exists because pollution kills people and damages the commons that no single private actor has incentive to protect. The first-term EPA wasn't just deregulating — it was transferring the cost of pollution from industry to the public.
