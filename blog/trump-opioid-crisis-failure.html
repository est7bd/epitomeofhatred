Trump Declared the Opioid Crisis a Public Health Emergency. Then Underfunded It by $11 Billion. Overdose Deaths Kept Rising.

On October 26, 2017, Trump declared the opioid crisis a national public health emergency from the White House — an announcement made with great ceremony and promises of action. The declaration unlocked exactly zero in new federal funding. Trump's own Commission on Combating Drug Addiction and the Opioid Crisis, chaired by New Jersey Governor Chris Christie, recommended $45 billion in emergency supplemental funding. The administration never provided it. Opioid overdose deaths rose from 42,249 in 2016 to 49,860 in 2019 — a 18% increase during Trump's watch. The same administration was simultaneously trying to repeal the Medicaid coverage and ACA protections that many people with addiction depended on for treatment access.

The specific legal status of Trump's October 2017 declaration matters. A "public health emergency" declaration under the Public Health Service Act provides administrative flexibilities and allows tapping a small emergency fund — the Public Health Emergency Fund, which at the time of Trump's declaration contained approximately $57,000, not billions. A true emergency response to the opioid crisis would have required either a major disaster declaration (which provides much larger federal resources) or supplemental congressional appropriations. Trump chose the weaker declaration and did not request the supplemental funding his own commission said was needed.

The cruel irony: the same administration that declared the opioid crisis an emergency was simultaneously pursuing policies that would have gutted the very programs that fund addiction treatment. Medicaid — which Trump's AHCA would have cut by $880 billion — funds approximately 40% of all addiction treatment in the United States. The mental health and substance abuse treatment benefits that the ACA required insurers to cover were what Trump was trying to eliminate. The people dying of opioid overdoses were disproportionately working-class, rural, and uninsured or Medicaid-dependent — the exact population that Trump's healthcare policy attacks were targeting. You cannot simultaneously declare a public health emergency and try to eliminate the public health insurance that treats it.
