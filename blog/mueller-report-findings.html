First Term Record — April 18, 2019 Mueller obstruction Russia fact-checked

The Mueller Report Found 10 Instances of Potential Obstruction of Justice and Documented Extensive Russia-Campaign Contacts. Barr Hid It for Nearly a Month.

Robert Mueller delivered his report to Attorney General William Barr on March 22, 2019. Barr waited four days, then sent Congress a four-page summary claiming the report cleared Trump. For the next 27 days, that summary was the public record — and Trump declared "total exoneration." When the redacted report was finally released April 18, it showed Barr had mischaracterized it. Mueller had documented 10 instances of potential obstruction of justice, more than 140 contacts between Trump campaign associates and Russian nationals, and had explicitly written that he could not exonerate Trump. Mueller then wrote Barr a letter saying his summary had "failed to capture the context, nature, and substance" of the investigation. A federal judge later called Barr's summary "misleading."

The Mueller Report is 448 pages long, covering two years of investigation into Russian interference in the 2016 election and whether Trump or his campaign obstructed that investigation. Volume I addressed Russian interference — concluding that Russia had interfered, that the campaign had benefited from that interference, but that the evidence did not establish a criminal conspiracy between the two. Volume II addressed obstruction of justice — and here the key paragraph is the one Barr's summary misrepresented. Mueller wrote: "If we had confidence after a thorough investigation of the facts that the President clearly did not commit obstruction of justice, we would so state. Based on the facts and the applicable legal standards, however, we are unable to reach that judgment."

"The summary letter the Department sent to Congress and released to the public late in the afternoon of March 24 did not fully capture the context, nature, and substance of this office's work and conclusions... There is now public confusion about critical aspects of the results of our investigation."

— Robert Mueller, in a letter to Attorney General William Barr, March 27, 2019 — five days after delivering the report and three days after Barr's four-page summary. Mueller was concerned enough to write formally.

The 10 potential obstruction instances Mueller documented include: Trump ordering White House Counsel Don McGahn to fire Mueller (McGahn refused); Trump asking FBI Director James Comey to drop the investigation into Michael Flynn; Trump firing Comey; Trump's attempts to limit the scope of the Mueller investigation; Trump dangling pardons in ways that could have influenced witness cooperation; and Trump's attempts to get Sessions to "unrecuse" from the investigation. In each case, Mueller laid out the evidence and the applicable legal standards, but declined to make a prosecutorial judgment — citing the DOJ policy that a sitting president cannot be indicted.
